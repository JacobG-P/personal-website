Introduction

In 2022, I was inspired to transform my brother's replica GTR Skyline model into a functioning remote control (RC) vehicle. I purchased initial components, including an RS-775 drill motor, an H-bridge for motor control, and a LiPo battery. However, I quickly realized that the limited space within the small replica model made the project impractical.

A year later, I acquired a 3D printer to revive the idea, this time creating an RC replica of my own car, a 1992 E36 318is Coupe. However, I decided to upgrade the project to an M3 model—it’s much cooler, after all!

Project Objectives

I had several overarching objectives for the RC project, including:

  • Remote Control: As the name implies, the vehicle must be fully remote-controlled, allowing distant communication and visuals.
  • Speed: The RC should be as fast as reasonably possible, considering cost and other limiting factors.
  • DIY Approach: The RC must be predominantly created by myself.
  • Automotive Features: The RC must incorporate additional features commonly found in typical automobiles.
  • Design: The RC should be sleek and compact, discreetly masking its features.

Project Overview

The project officially began in May 2023 and is still ongoing, encountering numerous challenges addressed through several iterations while gaining valuable insights in the process.

The initial focus was on creating the shell of the E36 by manipulating a 3D scan of an M3. Since its inception, the RC's design size has remained consistent. The shell was scaled to 400 mm in length, representing a 1:11.0825 scale of the real-life vehicle. This scale is somewhat unconventional, as later discovered when sourcing common RC parts. However, it was chosen to provide sufficient onboard space for electrical and mechanical components while also allowing the shell to be printed in two separate pieces. This approach accommodated the 3D printer’s bed size constraints of 220 mm, with the pieces later joined to assemble the full shell.

I will attempt to run you through, retrospectively, the iterations thus far of the E36 RC and Controller.

RC Iterations

Iteration 1.0

Iteration 1.0 included several revisions within versions. I’ve compiled all video recordings of the iterations and will try to detail as much as possible about the features included while recollecting the thought process behind certain implementations and/or changes.

Iteration 1.1

Iteration 1.1 began with the intention of minimizing costs which limited objectives and ultimately evolved into an expensive venture progressing through the project.

Iteration 1.1.1

This appears to be my first design. It seems like I gathered a good portion of the necessary parts without much detail or thought. The model uses the RS-775 drill motor, an H-bridge, and a stepper motor initially purchased. I also planned to implement a speaker, as evidenced by the black speaker case intended for a UART MP3 player and a 1W 8 ohm speaker previously created. It also incorporated an Arduino Nano and breadboard. At this stage, there was no intention to include suspension due to additional costs. The design features a basic rack-and-pinion system with minimal detail regarding its connection, except for its attachment to the stepper motor and drive. The power supply was a basic battery pack, assumed at the time to provide sufficient power but, in hindsight, was far from adequate. Overall, this iteration is an early concept design as I began to delve deeper into the project.

RC Iteration 1.1.1 CAD

Iteration 1.1.2

In this iteration, I appear to have done more research and spawned new ideas. Introducing the proposed telemetry through a LoRa SX 1278. The power system was expanded to include 2 x 9V batteries for new rear, front indicators, and front lights powered by 3V LEDs. The main power source was upgraded to what seems to be a 3S LiPo battery (though I’m not entirely certain).

I remember encountering difficulties determining motor specifications at the time. Additional levels were added to accommodate more components and optimize space constraints. The RS-775 motor was now mounted, driving a worm gear and a ~30-tooth spur gear to the drive shaft

RC Iteration 1.1.2 CAD

Iteration 1.1.3

This iteration introduced more ideas, some of which made it to the final design—such as the RC cameras. I purchased the 1000TVL cameras and TS582 transmitter seen in the model, complimented on the controller side with a RC832 receiver, 7” LCD, and AV LCD driver. The front camera was to be mounted on two servos which would rotate in yaw and pitch. However, the steering mechanism and cluster additions seen in this version were far too small to replicate with a 3D printer.

The cluster was modeled after an E36 dash and it was intended to be fully functioning. Tiny stepper motors were purchased to actuate the dials to fit behind. While, 0402 LEDs were purchased for backlighting and indicators. Speed and RPM would be measured at the shaft via a photo interrupter and slotted disc connected to the shaft. While, fuel and temp would interpret shunt resistor and DHT11 sensor readings. Meanwhile, the steering wheel would be connected to the steering mechanism to turn in tandem with the stepper.

Iteration 1.3

This iteration focused on refining details in the existing design, including a more refined front suspension setup and control arms. Suspension components that were eventually purchased were introduced in this iteration, 1603-BZ01 (red shocks).

A controllable spoiler, operated by a servo, was added but later discontinued and never tested. The differential also took a more feasible form. The LiPo batteries and ESCs were selected, purchased and designed into the model

Iteration 1.4

Significant progress was made. The rack-and-pinion mechanism was replaced with M2 threaded rods, tie rods, and a servo for control. Front control arms were designed but proved too flexible due to insufficient lateral constraints. A roll cage was added.

Two 12W mini speakers, amplifier, 1S batteries were purchased and included in the model. Iteration 2.1

A gear case was added to reduce debris and allow for lubrication. The drive mechanism was straightened, using an M0.5 worm gear to transfer power to a spur gear.

RC Iteration 2.1 CAD

Iteration 2.2

Testing revealed that the suspension was too weak, with the mounts poorly positioned to handle force loading. To improve leverage, the mounts were relocated closer to the tires.

The shocks had a measured spring rate of approximately 285.7 g/mm and required two shocks per wheel to prevent the RC vehicle from bottoming out. Calculations were performed to optimize the spring ratios for the front and rear suspension based on weight, center of gravity (COG), and loading conditions, manipulating shock angle and mounting position.

Additionally, an initial concept for the front suspension involved stacking two springs, as housing both shocks side by side proved challenging with the steering mechanism.

Iteration 2.2 Drivetrain Testing

This iteration experienced multiple drivetrain and gear failures. PLA gears, as seen in the images, were unsuitable and required frequent replacement due to excessive wear. The gears endured significant stress, rotating at high RPMs, with inefficiencies generating heat that worsened wear.

The drivetrain used a brass M.5 Worm Gear, which exhibited contact issues with the driven gear. These were improved through tighter tolerances and by clamping the top of the driven case. Additionally, securing the gears was challenging and required hot glue.

Iteration 2.5

The drivetrain was redesigned with a cog-and-belt setup to address backlash and vibration issues. Motor positions were made adjustable with hand-tightened bolts. The video also captures the height adjustability of the drivetrain, which was actually introduced in Iteration 2.6.

RC Iteration 2.5 CAD

Iteration 2.5 Testing

Iteration 2.5 Remote Control Testing

The control system was now functional enough to allow for remote operation and driving of the vehicle. The camera features were set up and powered by the motor’s LiPo battery, providing sufficient current for snappy and consistent movement. The camera transmitted an onboard display of the RC vehicle to the controller’s LCD. However, the LCD was partially broken, likely due to mishandling at some point.

The RC vehicle experienced significant traction issues on the smooth wooden floor when using the 3D-printed TPU VarioShore filament tires. The 3D-printed tires were softer and grippier than standard 3D-printed plastics, thanks to VarioShore, which incorporates baking soda that expands and softens when printed at higher temperatures. Despite this, the 3D-printed tires were preferred for their customizability and self-made design but require softer materials through off-the-shelf tires.

Another persistent issue is the drivetrain creating significant vibration. While less apparent in this iteration, the RC vehicle has been equipped with a slightly modified drivetrain that uses cogs and belts to eliminate backlash. The belt tension is adjustable via motor mounting positions. However, the drivetrain is still not perfectly straight, with slight misalignment and fitment issues.

Controller Iterations

Controller Iteration 1.0

The initial concept design for the remote control began with implementing the desired features. A 7” LCD was integrated with its driver connected. Left and right joysticks were added to control specific functions: the left joystick managed steering, drive, and hazard lights, while the right joystick controlled the camera view position (yaw and pitch) and horn feature. A single potentiometer was included, likely intended to control the music volume, along with three buttons presumed to handle skip, rewind, and pause music functionalities.

The control was taking shape. Arduino Nano microboards were configured as the transmitter and receiver, each connected to a respective transceiver. An H-bridge was implemented, apparently not connected to a motor at this stage. It seems this was intended to actuate the LCD angle. The LCD would be connected to a rail system and worm drive mechanism. The bottom of the LCD appeared to be a pivot point, with the rail connected to the top portion of the LCD via ball bearings, enabling angular adjustments. Power was sourced from multiple batteries: two 3S LiPos would power the RC832 and LCD, one 1S LiPo powered the transceivers, and two coin cell batteries would power the microcontrollers. The controller was 3D-printed, and while the LCD was tested, it required additional space for proper functionality.

Controller Iteration 1.0 CAD

Controller Iteration 2.0

Iteration 2.0 introduced a larger, more detailed design. The controller consisted of two compartments to accommodate the required wiring and components. The top plate, featuring joysticks and buttons, began to resemble a PlayStation controller. The button layout was conceptualised as follows: Left-left: Left indicator, Left-up: Music track up, Left-right: Right indicator, Left-down: Music track down, Right-left: Switch tachometer LCD screens, Right-up: LCD angle up, Right-right: Spare (now RMC2 reset), Right-down: LCD angle down.

An additional potentiometer was added to adjust the brightness of the RC LEDs. Indicator buttons were paired with orange 0402 LEDs to visually display their states.

The tachometer LCD would display on-board data collected by the RC Transmitter Microcontroller Board (TMC2) and transmitted via a receiver to the Receiver Microcontroller Board (RMC2).

A control panel for the LCD was added (not shown in the video), allowing adjustment of LCD settings. Cutouts were made for RC832 wires to pass through compartments, as space constraints necessitated external routing.

The LCD mechanism was upgraded to include a 3–6V micro-geared DC motor. This motor, secured to a mount with screws, drove a shaft connecting separate left and right worm gears, which engaged with the toothed arms of the LCD. The worm gears were designed to allow the user to manually override the motor, utilizing the higher gear ratio to adjust the LCD’s position via dials. However, a significant issue was identified: the worm gears were not reversible. While the motor could drive the worm gears using an H-bridge and buttons, the design was tested and found to be ineffective. The LCD was not correctly positioned to maintain constant contact between its toothed arms and the worm gears.

1S LiPo batteries were replaced with coin cell batteries that were thought to be sufficient, however, later switched back to LiPo batteries that had the required current. Electrical components were connected and tested including the top plate buttons, joystick and potentiometer.

Controller Iteration 3.0

In this version, the controller underwent reorganization and was fully assembled and functional, though it revealed several issues that would be addressed in Iteration 4.0.

This iteration catered towards space efficiency and organization by adding baffles to separate and store RC832 and LCD driver cables more effectively. Additional cutouts were introduced to facilitate easier wire routing. Coin cell batteries were replaced with 1S LiPos and a 9V battery for the H-bridge. Tension brackets were added to address gaps caused by 3D printing imperfections.

The LCD mechanism was slightly adjusted, repositioning the LCD to ensure it could pivot and remain in contact with a spur gear instead of the worm gear. The manual control was still in the design. However, issues arose from 3D printing inaccuracies: the controller cutouts interfered with the LCD arms, the arms were difficult to print, and securing the motor shaft in the resin-printed shaft proved challenging. Despite these LCD-related problems, assembly of the controller continued.

The RMC2 and TMC2 microcontrollers were wired and coded, enabling remote control of the vehicle and onboard diagnostics for the RC, which was not yet connected.

Controller Iteration 4.0

This iteration brought several optimisations.

Controller Iteration 4.0 CAD with Relay

Controller Iteration 4.0 CAD Current

Space

Although spacious in CAD, the previous design lacked sufficient room during assembly seen with the clutter of wires and cabling. The controller was made slightly wider and taller, incorporating racks for unused vertical space. RC832 cables can now run inside the controller.

Organization

Component placement was previously dictated by space constraints, making assembly and disassembly cumbersome. In the new design:

  • All batteries are stored in the bottom compartment near switches.
  • Transceivers and microcontrollers are positioned closer to buttons and sensors, reducing wiring complexity.
  • The bottom compartment now includes push-connector terminals, enabling quick disconnection and reassembly.

Battery Levels

Monitoring battery levels is now simpler. Batteries are accessible externally through cutouts covered with pop-out panels, allowing users to check voltages with a voltmeter or replace batteries with ease.

Fewer Batteries

The number of batteries was reduced by running similar power supplies in parallel, improving both space and wiring.

Battery Converters

Faulty step-up converters from the previous design caused voltage spikes. These have been replaced with reliable step-up converters, allowing 1S LiPo batteries to power the microcontrollers at 5V.

Diagnostics

Previously, diagnosing microcontroller freezes was challenging. Each microcontroller now includes an LCD to display status updates, showing code progress and connection states.

Microcontroller Access

Both microcontrollers are now housed in the same compartment, with cutouts for easy cable connections, diagnostics, and firmware updates.

New LCD

The previous 7” LCD broke and displayed only half the screen. It was replaced with a fully functional 7” LCD and a 1080 x 720 resolution driver. An AV-to-HDMI converter processes the RC camera feed.

LCD Mechanism

The LCD angle adjustment mechanism was simplified. Manual adjustment via worm gears was removed, leaving motorized adjustments controlled by buttons.

Code Changes

The updated code now bypasses connection hold points, allowing the controller to function independently while maintaining compatibility with the RC system.

The controller initially used an electrical relay to switch loads and interpret voltage levels through a stunt sensor. However, this approach was removed, and it was concluded that these tasks would be completed manually using cutouts and a voltmeter.
